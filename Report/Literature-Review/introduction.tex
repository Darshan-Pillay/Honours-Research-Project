\section{The problem of plant diseases}
Our lives would not be possible without agriculture. We use goods like medicine, pesticides, and rubber everyday, all of which are made from plants. Plants are ubiquitous in our lives, and they fulfill some of our essential needs. Most importantly, plants feed most people globally \cite{mccouchFeedingFuture2013}. But many of us still suffer from hunger. In 2024, it was estimated that between 638 and 720 million people did not have enough to eat \cite{StateFoodSecurity2025}. Plant diseases make this situation even worse by robbing us of food \cite{carvajal-yepesGlobalSurveillanceSystem2019, heHighThroughputRoboticPhenotyping2025, nazarovInfectiousPlantDiseases2020, hughesOpenAccessRepository2016}. Therefore, they are a direct threat to our livelihoods.
\subparagraph{}
Plant diseases make it difficult for crops to survive and stay healthy \cite{agriosINTRODUCTION2005}. They interfere with a plant's cells causing them to malfunction or die \cite{huberRelationshipNutritionPlant2012}. And each cell helps the plant carry out essential life-sustaining functions: conducting photosynthesis, and absorbing water and nutrients. So, when these cells are damaged, the plant's ability to obtain and use nutrients is limited. As a result, plant diseases stunt the growth, and decrease the yield and nutritional value of crops \cite{savaryCropLossesDue2012, vyskaTradeoffDiseaseResistance2016, chinPlantDiseaseDetection2023, kitpoEarlyRiceDisease2018, martinelliAdvancedMethodsPlant2015}. In some cases, the damage is so severe that farmers lose up to 100\% of their crops \cite{nazarovInfectiousPlantDiseases2020, dongPlantPADPlatformLargescale2024}. However, the losses caused by plant diseases goes further than just the yield and quality of crops; they extend to rural communities, consumers, the environment, and governments \cite{savaryCropLossesDue2012}. Therefore, plant diseases threaten the health of crops, and this has disastrous far-reaching effects on our livelihoods. Clearly, we need solutions to deal with them.

\section{Some solutions to plant disease}
Luckily, several options exist. For example, plant breeding is an environmentally sustainable and cost-effective solution, which helps to produce disease-resistant varieties of plants \cite{anandBreedingTechniquesDispense2023, ortonIntroduction2020, heHighThroughputRoboticPhenotyping2025, furbankPhenomicsTechnologiesRelieve2011}. Alternatively, we also resort to precision agriculture, which uses data from crops and their environment to make optimal decisions about agricultural actions \cite{PrecisionAgricultureFood2025, PlantDiseaseDetection, cisternasSystematicLiteratureReview2020, yandunnarvaezSurveyRangingImaging2017}. For instance, precision agriculture aids farmers in deciding on the best way to apply pesticides during a pathogen epidemic \cite{bockPlantDiseaseSeverity2010} by helping determine when to apply pesticides, how much pesticide to apply, and where to apply it; as a result, such precise pesticide application will reduce costs, save resources, and benefit the environment and human health \cite{pathakCurrentStatusPesticide2022, reiser3DImagingSonar2017, khalidRealTimePlantHealth2023, qiaoEditorialAISensors2022, dechantAutomatedIdentificationNorthern2017, HelpingFarmersFace2025}. So, plant breeding and precision agriculture help deal with plant diseases; however, these methods depend on effective plant phenotyping \cite{fahlgrenVersatilePhenotypingSystem2015, furbankPhenomicsTechnologiesRelieve2011a, gehanPlantCVV2Image2017, zhangBOCNNBiLSTMDeepLearning2024, zhuDeepLearningbasedText2025a, anandBreedingTechniquesDispense2023}.

\subsection{The relationship between plant diseases and plant phenotyping}
Plant phenotyping is the evaluation of observable or measurable plant phenotypes \cite{fioraniFutureScenariosPlant2013, johannsenGenotypeConceptionHeredity1911a}, which are proxy measurements or traits indicating a plant's stress, health or growth in relation to its environment \cite{fioraniFutureScenariosPlant2013, liReviewImagingTechniques2014}. Moreover, phenotypes represent traits of an individual plant in terms of all of its chemical, physiological, morphological, and behavioral aspects \cite{guoPhenotypingPlants2014, mohammedReviewPlantPhenotyping2025, liReviewImagingTechniques2014}. So, plant phenotyping provides quantitative data that represents various aspects of a plant. When a plant is diseased its form, function and integrity are affected \cite{agriosINTRODUCTION2005}, therefore reliable measurements of all aspects of the plant are required in plant disease solutions. Consequently, plant phenotyping is an effective quantitative method for plant breeding and precision agriculture \cite{gehanPlantCVV2Image2017, wenThermalImagingDigital2023, dongPlantPADPlatformLargescale2024, mutkaImagebasedPhenotypingPlant2015a, anandBreedingTechniquesDispense2023}.

\subsubsection{Plant phenotyping and plant breeding}
The goal of plant breeding is to develop plants with desirable traits like disease resistance. In this regard, the most important tasks for breeders are the selection of suitable individual plants and the development of genetic material \cite{anandBreedingTechniquesDispense2023}. Plant phenotyping assists in these tasks. It does so by providing breeders data about a plant's phenotype-environment interactions, which allows them to identify and select suitable parameters for the development of improved crops \cite{xiaoAdvancedHighthroughputPlant2022}. And this method has been used successfully in several breeding programmes for disease resistance. To name a few, \cite{visioniGenomeWideAssociation2018} conducted a study for Barley Rust Stripe resistance, \cite{zhuIdentificationQuantitativeTrait2003} for Oat Crown Rust resistance, \cite{pumphreyValidatingFhb1QTL2007} for Wheat Head Blight resistance, \cite{mcclureGenomeWideAssociationStudy2018} for Apple Scab resistance, and \cite{yangGbvdr6GeneEncoding2018} for Cotton Verticillium Wilt resistance. Thus, plant phenotyping effectively assists plant breeding programmes.

\subsubsection{Plant phenotyping and precision agriculture}
Precision agriculture provides data-driven decision making for optimal agricultural management, and plant phenotyping can enable this \cite{zhuDeepLearningbasedText2025, qiaoEditorialAISensors2022, reiser3DImagingSonar2017, dechantAutomatedIdentificationNorthern2017, bergerPrecisionAgricultureEnabler2022}. Notably, there are many pesticide, disease detection and warning systems that rely on phenotyping \cite{khalidRealTimePlantHealth2023, kitpoEarlyRiceDisease2018}. For example, \cite{caffiEvaluationWarningSystem2010} discusses a warning system for controlling infections of Grapevine Downy Mildew, and \cite{bergerZoneSelectivePlantProtection2022} presents an early disease detection system for selective pesticide application which successfully helped managed diseases in apple, olive and almond farms. Evidently, precision agriculture with phenotyping provides effective plant disease management. But traditional phenotyping has problems.

\subsection{Tradtional plant phenotyping solutions, and their challenges}
Human naked eye observation is the main traditional phenotyping method used in plant disease detection. This method needs continuous effort from human raters which can be expensive. And in poorer countries, raters are far and few between \cite{rameshPlantDiseaseDetection2018, basavaiahTomatoLeafDisease2020, sambanaEfficientPlantDisease2025}, so farmers will need to expend more time, energy and resources to obtain raters \cite{alhiaryFastAccurateDetection2011, DetectionClassificationLeaf2025, gillComprehensiveReviewHigh2022}. Thus the consultation of human experts is expensive, time-consuming and not accessible to everyone. Additionally, naked eye observation has practical limitations. For example, when plant samples are needed, visual assessment is destructive, and this prevents the observation of plant traits over time \cite{gehanPlantCVV2Image2017, gillComprehensiveReviewHigh2022, hangClassificationPlantLeaf2019}. Moreover, visual assessment is error prone when a disease does not induce any visible symptoms in a plant, thus the detection of early disease onset is difficult for human raters. However, alternative laboratory methods for early disease detection exist \cite{arnalbarbedoDigitalImageProcessing2013}, but they can also be computationally expensive and time consuming just like human visual assessment \cite{basavaiahTomatoLeafDisease2020, rameshPlantDiseaseDetection2018, bockPlantDiseaseSeverity2010, PlantDiseaseDetection}. Fortunatley, image sensing technologies combined with machine learning and image processing techniques can overcome many problems that traditional phenotyping methods suffer from.

\subsection{Image sensing technologies an alternative to traditional phenotyping methods}
Image sensing technologies overcome some of the deficiencies of traditional plant phenotyping methods. For example, traditional methods can be destructive, which complicates the measurement of plant traits over time \cite{gehanPlantCVV2Image2017, gillComprehensiveReviewHigh2022, hangClassificationPlantLeaf2019}. And measuring phenotypes over time is important because plant disease symptoms can be latent or appear suddenly, and then progress rapidly \cite{mutkaImagebasedPhenotypingPlant2015a, chenHyperspectralImagingdrivenDeep2025}. Thus, non-destructive phenotyping methods are beneficial for early disease detection and treatment. Evidently, traditional methods are lacking in this regard, but image sensing technologies are not, since they can precisely and non-destrutively sample large populations \cite{benchangzhang1UtilizingHighThroughputPhenotyping2024, heHighThroughputRoboticPhenotyping2025}. Moreover, this can be done in a very short period of time \cite{daschoudhuryLeveragingImageAnalysis2019}. Therefore, image sensing technology is more economical than time-consuming human labour. 

\subparagraph{}
Sensors also capture plant tissue–light interactions and this enables the measurement and quantification of disease symptoms that are not visible to the eye \cite{liReviewImagingTechniques2014}. Specifically, plant tissues absorb, reflect, emit, transmit, and fluoresce different wavelengths of light \cite{liuPhotosyntheticPhysiologyBlue2021, gatesSpectralPropertiesPlants1965}. Light is captured by a sensor, and this light can have different wavelengths each of which may correspond to distinct biochemical or physiological aspects of a plant \cite{walshAdvancementsImagingSensors2024, chopraHyperspectralImagingCharacterize2025}. Therefore, sensing technologies increase the possible types of traits we can observe, which allows for earlier, more accurate, and comprehensive disease symptom detection. 

\subparagraph{}
Numerous studies have demonstrated the effectiveness of sensing technology in plant disease detection and management.  \cite{burlingQuantumYieldNonregulated2010} used chlorophyll fluorescence imaging to detect leaf rust and assess its severity in wheat.  \cite{chaerleThermalChlorophyllFluorescenceImaging2004} used thermography and chlorophyll fluorescence imaging to non-destructively visualize \emph{Cercospora beticola} infections. \cite{yanyangEarlyDetectionRice2012} applies a near-infrared hyper-spectral image to detect blast rice in Nipponbare seedlings. \cite{xieDetectionEarlyBlight2015} used hyperspectral imaging to detect early blight and late blight diseases in tomato leaves. \cite{yangTeaDiseasesDetection2019} uses thermal image sensing to detect tea diseases. Clearly, image sensors have proven effective in supporting plant disease detection and management. Furthermore, in recent years, a wide range of machine learning and image processing techniques have been used with these sensors.

\section{Image processing pipelines}
In spite of varying characterizations of an archetypal image processing pipeline, most existing solutions in the literatures exhibit common element or phases in which machine learning and various other techniques are applied to assist in the detection of plant diseases \cite{kitpoEarlyRiceDisease2018, khalidRealTimePlantHealth2023, camargoImageprocessingBasedAlgorithm2009, rahatPrecisionAgricultureTea2025, basavaiahTomatoLeafDisease2020, wenThermalImagingDigital2023, kumbharCropDiseaseDetection, kaurPlantsDiseaseIdentification2019, perez-sanzPlantPhenomicsOverview2017, alhiaryFastAccurateDetection2011, desaiEnhancingImageQuality2025}. These phases are, namely, image acquisition through sensors which we have discussed earlier, image preprocessing, image segmentation, feature extraction, and  classification. \cite{kitpoEarlyRiceDisease2018, khalidRealTimePlantHealth2023, camargoImageprocessingBasedAlgorithm2009, rahatPrecisionAgricultureTea2025, basavaiahTomatoLeafDisease2020, wenThermalImagingDigital2023}

\subsection{Preprocessing}
Image preprocessing involves noise removal, image enhancement, and colour space conversion. During image acquisition, noise originating from the imaging sensor, the environment, or other external sources can compromise the quality of the data. This is problematic because such artifacts can ultimately reduce the accuracy of downstream image processing and classification phases. Therefore, preprocessing is crucial for accurate and effective plant disease detection.

\subparagraph{} 
Filtering is a common technique used in preprocessing to remove salt-and-pepper noise while preserving sharp edges or disease spots \cite{gonzalezDigitalImageProcessing2017, saiImageQualityEnhancement2023, thangaduraiComputerVisionimageEnhancement2014}. Common examples in the literature include median filtering \cite{hamudaSurveyImageProcessing2016a, desaiEnhancingImageQuality2025}, Gaussian filtering \cite{mamathaPreprocessingTomatoLeaf2024}, and bilateral filtering \cite{aBenchmarkingImageFiltering2025}. In addition to noise removal, image enhancement techniques like histogram equalization are used to improve the visibility of disease spots that might otherwise be obscured by poor lighting. Specifically, this method improves the contrast between healthy and diseased areas of an image \cite{EnhancingTomatoLeaf, thangaduraiComputerVisionimageEnhancement2014a, patilEnhancedRadialBasis2022}.

\subparagraph{} Another technique is colour space conversion which is necessary to isolate relevant plant traits. Typically, images are captured in RGB, but many studies convert them to HSI (Hue, Saturation, Intensity) or the Lab* color space. This conversion is preferred because it is easier to identify yellow or diseased spots when color information is separated from luminance \cite{luna-benosoDetectionDiseasesTomato2021, heTomatoDiseaseDegree2023, khanIntelligentFastSystem2024, hussainRobustLeafDisease2024, kumbharCropDiseaseDetection, krusePixelClassificationMethods2014}. Consequently, by utilizing these preprocessing techniques, researchers can ensure that subsequent inage processing phases, such as image segmentation, are performed on high-quality, reliable data. And this leads to more effective and reliable results.

\subsection{Segmentation}
Segmentation is a key image-processing task common to many image-based plant phenotyping solutions. It is the process of separating the object of interest from its background or other parts of the image which are not relevant.  Often statistical metrics, and features are only desired for the object of interest. Hence including image data unrelated to the object of interest affects the accuracy and reliability of any results obtained from downstream processes in the pipeline. Therefore, the validity and precision of the derived phenotypical measurements and analysis depend on the image segmentation method selected \cite{choudhurySegmentationTechniquesChallenges2020, muller-linowPlantScreenMobile2019}. Therefore, image segmentation methods are integral to image processing in plant phenotyping.

\subparagraph{}
Selecting the appropriate segmentation technique is crucial when considering the type of phenotypic parameter to be measured. Segmentation methods are typically classifiable as traditional or learning-based \cite{choudhurySegmentationTechniquesChallenges2020}. Traditional segmentation methods typically target holistic phenotypical traits like plant height and include frame differencing \cite{daschoudhuryHolisticComponentPlant2018}, colour-based \cite{hamudaSurveyImageProcessing2016a}, edge detection \cite{wangImageSegmentationOverlapping2018}, spectral band difference-based \cite{gampaDatadrivenApproachDetecting2019}, shape modelling-based \cite{chenLeafSegmentationFunctional2019}, and graph-based segmentation \cite{praveenkumarImageBasedLeaf2019}. In contrast to traditional techniques, learning-based methods, which utilise machine learning or deep learning \cite{papeUtilizingMachineLearning2015, abdallaFinetuningConvolutionalNeural2019}, are more suitable for targeting component-based traits of specific organs or parts of a plant \cite{choudhurySegmentationTechniquesChallenges2020}.

\subsection{Feature Extraction And Classification}
Feature extraction gathers information from targeted regions of plant images, allowing for the analysis or classification of this data to answer questions or guide decisions about the area of interest. In plant disease detection, we extract features from plant images to quantify actual phenotypic traits, and this helps to determine if a tomato plant leaf shows signs of disease or not.

\subsubsection{A Taxonomy of Phenotypical Traits}
\paragraph{}
A phenotypical trait is classifiable as morphological, physiological, or temporal. Additionally, the literature indicates morphological and physiological phenotypical traits are classifiable as holistic-based or component-based \cite{daschoudhuryLeveragingImageAnalysis2019}. Holistic properties consider the plant as a whole, whereas component-based features examine individual parts of the organism, such as its fruit, flower, leaves, stem, etc. 

\subsubsection{Leaf-Derived Phenotypical Traits: Robust Proxy Indicators of Plant Metabolic and Physiological Status}
\paragraph{}
Leaf-derived traits are among the most used indicators of plant status because leaves rapidly respond to environmental stimuli and robustly indicate various factors that determine plant health, growth, and stress \cite{zhangHighthroughputPhenotypingPlant2023}. Traits of leaves can monitor and predict a host of plant health, growth and stress factors. For example, leaf area predicts plant productivity because reduced leaf area results in decreased chlorophyll content and less photosynthesis. Therefore, leaf area is a good proxy measure for evaluating plant growth and has been used to monitor and estimate crop yield and health \cite{zhangLeafAreaIndex2021}.

\paragraph{}
Various other leaf morphological traits exist in plant phenotyping. These include number of leaves  and inclination angle for monitoring plant growth and development \cite{praveenkumarRosettePlantSegmentation2020, zhangHighthroughputPhenotypingPlant2023}, and leaf thickness for measuring abiotic stressors like water scarcity \cite{afzalLeafThicknessPredict2017}. Additionally, physiological traits of leaves exist that indicate various factors of plant status, such as stomatal conductance for detecting biotic stressors like pathogens \cite{ishimweApplicationsThermalImaging2014} and leaf water content for drought stress \cite{watanabeFundamentalStudyWater2022}. Considering the above, leaf-derived phenotypical traits are robust proxies of various metabolic and physiological plant parameters.

\paragraph{}
Although leaves play an important role in evaluating plant growth, health and resilience to stress, these evaluations cannot be scaled to the whole plant \cite{zhangHighthroughputPhenotypingPlant2023}. Rather, other parts of the plant should be phenotyped to understand the processes governing plant status comprehensively. The literature is rife with such examples that study traits of roots \cite{bodnerHyperspectralImagingNovel2018}, stems \cite{daschoudhuryAutomatedStemAngle2017}, seeds \cite{zhangHighThroughputPhenotypingSeed2018}, and fruit \cite{abebeImageBasedHighThroughputPhenotyping2023}.

\section{Current State of Image-based Tomato Plant Leaf Phenotyping}

In the literature there is indication of two approaches  for plant leaf image disease detection \cite{ahmedLeafImagebasedPlant2021a}. The first approach is feature-based which requires the development of hand-crafted features such as color and textural features to train a traditional machine learning algorithm. And the second is deep-learning based in which features are automatically learnt.

\subsection{Feature-based Disease Detection}
There are many different types of features used in the literature and include textural and color-based features. For example, there are studies which calculate textural statistics like the Gray level co-occurrence matrices, and Hu moments which are then used to detect plant diseases \cite{haralickTexturalFeaturesImage1973, kimClassificationGrapefruitPeel, arivazhaganDetectionUnhealthyRegion2013, ahmedLeafImagebasedPlant2021a, mokhtarSVMBasedDetectionTomato2015}. In terms of color features some common types of features are color histograms, color moments, and correlograms \cite{patilPlantLeafDisease2024, huangContentbasedImageRetrieval2010, kimClassificationGrapefruitPeel}.

\paragraph{}
Once features are determined a suitable classifier is constructed, and typically this is a traditional machine learning algorithm. There are many different types of classifiers used in the literature in leaf disease detection. For example, \cite{hussainRobustLeafDisease2024} uses Fuzzy sets and achieved an accuracy of $97.1\%$. \cite{mokhtarSVMBasedDetectionTomato2015} employed a Support Vector Machine classifier with textural features from Gray level co-occurrence matrices  to detect tomato leaf diseases and achieved an accuracy of $99\%$. \cite{basavaiahTomatoLeafDisease2020} used a Naive bayes classifier and achieved an accuracy of $86\%$. \cite{govardhanDiagnosisTomatoPlant2019a} used the method of Random Forest and achieved an accuracy of $95\%$. \cite{joshiClassificationTomatoLeaf2023} used K Nearest Neighbours classifier to detect tomato leaf diseases and achieved $93\%$ accuracy. \cite{sabrolIntensityBasedFeature2016} used a decision tree to detect tomato plant diseases and achieved $78\%$ accuracy. Evidently, feature-based plant leaf disease detection is effective. However many studies use data sets like PlantVillage to train and validate their model, and this data set is imbalanced with images taken in uniform controlled conditions. Therefore, further investigation into the generalizability of these models is required to determine their efficacy on unseen data and in field scenarios.

\subsection{Deep learning Disease Detection}
In terms of deep learning approaches there are numerous different architectures, and solutions used. Notably there is an extensive use of Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) with many different types of architecture used, and accuracy rates as high as $98\%$ \cite{jelaliDeepLearningNetworksbased2024a, wangEfficientDeepLearning2024, baserTomConvImprovedCNN2023, chenUsingHybridConvolutional2024, fuentesHighPerformanceDeepNeural2018, natarajanDetectionDiseaseTomato2020}. Despite the widespread use of CNNs and their success in plant tomato plant leaf disease detection, many studies train models on data sets which are imbalanced and with images taken in uniform lighting conditions. Consequently, this means that proposed models may not generalize well to unseen data or practical applications in the field. 

\section{Miscellaneous Issues Of Image-Based Phenotyping Systems}
\subsection{Low-cost Automated Solutions In Greenhouses}
\paragraph{}
Many automated phenotypical image processing systems in greenhouses employ various advanced technologies, such as robotics \cite{saddikMappingAgriculturalSoil}, automated conveyor belts, and benchtop configurations \cite{bruningDevelopmentHyperspectralDistribution2019, peironeAssessingEfficiencyPhenotyping2018}, as well as advanced image sensors in high-throughput phenotyping systems \cite{liHighThroughputPlantPhenotyping2021}. However, these solutions are not accessible to all plant growers because they tend to be costly \cite{lienLowcostOpensourcePlatform2019, tovarRaspberryPiPowered2018a, minerviniImageAnalysisNew2015} Moreover, many systems are patented by specialized companies, rendering the hardware and software inflexible and unmodifiable \cite{czedik-eysenbergPhenoBoxFlexibleAutomated2018}. Furthermore, existing low-cost phenotyping systems in greenhouses have bespoke image analysis and processing pipelines, which prevent them from being generalized and used in broader contexts \cite{arnalbarbedoDigitalImageProcessing2013, liReviewImagingTechniques2014}. Therefore, there is a need for low-cost, flexible, modifiable, generalizable, and extendable automated image processing systems for plant phenotyping applications.

\subsection{Open-source Software, Systems and Affordable Hardware As Cost Reducers}
Recent studies in low-cost automated image-based plant phenotyping show increased development of open-source phenotyping systems and the use of readily accessible commercial off-the-shelf hardware \cite{liDigitizationVisualizationGreenhouse2015, minerviniImageAnalysisNew2015} to reduce the costs of image-based phenotyping in greenhouse environments. Cheap low-cost compute sensors called Raspberry PIs have been used with affordable RGB and hyperspectral sensors to monitor phenotypic traits in greenhouse environments \cite{tovarRaspberryPiPowered2018a}. Additionally, there has been an increase in open-source image-processing plant phenotyping systems like Phenobox \cite{czedik-eysenbergPhenoBoxFlexibleAutomated2018}. Despite these advances, several challenges remain in low-cost plant phenotyping systems.

\subsection{Challenges  In Automated Image-Based Plant Phenotyping}

\subsubsection{Large Image Sensor Data Throughput Necessitates Robust Image Processing Tools}
\paragraph{}
Modern image sensors in image-based phenotyping generate massive amounts of data \cite{gomesComputationalSustainabilityComputational2009}, necessitating the development of efficient image processing pipelines in these contexts to handle large datasets in the shortest possible time while also delivering precise and accurate phenotypical analysis \cite{minerviniImageAnalysisNew2015}.  Therefore, effective choices in image sensors are crucial for developing low-cost, automated image-based phenotyping systems \cite{zhangHighthroughputPhenotypingPlant2023}.

\subsubsection{The Effects of Image Sensor Choice On Phenotypical Analysis And Processing}
\paragraph{}
The choice of sensor has a non-trivial influence on the image analysis and processing techniques used in plant phenotyping and additionally also determines the breadth of traits that can be analysed \cite{liReviewImagingTechniques2014}. For example, hyperspectral imaging is practical for studying plant metabolic status; however, the sensors required generate Terabytes of data when monitoring multiple plants \cite{bauckhageDataMiningPattern2013}. Consequently, in low-cost settings, hyperspectral imaging of component-based phenotypical traits becomes infeasible because learning-based image techniques, which are most suitable for this purpose, are costly, time-consuming, and impractical when large amounts of phenotypical data are processed \cite{bauckhageDataMiningPattern2013}. Additionally, low-cost hardware has constrained bandwidth, storage, and compute power. Therefore, remote cloud image processing and analysis are required \cite{debaucheCloudArchitecturePlant2020}, which can be expensive \cite{minerviniImageAnalysisNew2015}.

\subsubsection{Solutions, and Limitations For Low-cost Large Data Throughput Image Processing Systems}
\subparagraph{}
In low-cost settings, commonly used strategies to manage the issues of large-scale image analysis and processing of phenotypic data include low-complexity traditional segmentation methods \cite{minerviniImageAnalysisNew2015}, the use of more affordable and straightforward imaging sensing technologies in low-cost off-the-shelf hardware \cite{tovarRaspberryPiPowered2018a}, and image compression techniques \cite{minerviniApplicationawareImageCompression2013}. However, each of these strategies comes with trade-offs in terms of precision, accuracy, and phenotypic analysis capabilities. Low-complexity traditional segmentation methods struggle to handle component-based phenotyping of specific organs in plants while requiring fewer computational resources \cite{choudhurySegmentationTechniquesChallenges2020}. RGB sensors are affordable and generate less raw data than other methods, such as hyperspectral imaging; however, their use is limited to the phenotyping of physiological plant traits \cite{liReviewImagingTechniques2014}. Image compression techniques can be used to reduce the amount of data transmission required for remote analysis but have a non-negligible impact on the accuracy of image segmentation \cite{minerviniApplicationawareImageCompression2013}. Therefore, a delicate balance must be struck between cost and numerous factors that affect the overall precision, accuracy, and measurability of phenotypic traits \cite{muller-linowPlantScreenMobile2019}.

\section{Potential Advances For Low-Cost Automated Image-Based Plant Phenotyping}
Numerous opportunities exist to improve the overall affordability and generalisability of plant phenotyping in automated greenhouse environments. Low-cost plant phenotyping is not a nascent field of inquiry. However, many tracts of innovation have not been fully exploited. For instance, learning-based imaging techniques for analysis and processing can reduce analysis and segmentation costs but struggle when executed on resource-constrained hardware \cite{nabwireReviewApplicationArtificial2021}. Therefore, there is a need for the development of efficient and optimised deep-learning or machine-learning models in resource-constrained environments. Similarly, many existing phenotyping systems are not generalizable to other plant species \cite{liReviewImagingTechniques2014, arvidssonGrowthPhenotypingPipeline2011a} and rely on proprietary software or hardware \cite{czedik-eysenbergPhenoBoxFlexibleAutomated2018}. Hence, there are opportunities to develop modifiable and flexible image-processing pipelines that are amenable to a broader variety of plant phenotyping contexts. Additionally, there are not many mobile applications for plant phenotyping. Mobile devices have advanced image sensors and can locally run image analysis and processing tasks, eliminating the need for cloud computing resources that increase phenotyping costs \cite{muller-linowPlantScreenMobile2019}.
